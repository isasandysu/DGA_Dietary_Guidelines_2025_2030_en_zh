Eat the Right Amount for You

  • The calories you need depend on your age, sex, height, weight, and level of physical activity.
  • Pay attention to portion sizes, particularly for foods and beverages higher in calories.
  • Hydration is a key factor in overall health. Choose water (still or sparkling) and unsweetened beverages.

Prioritize Protein Foods at Every Meal

  • Prioritize high-quality, nutrient-dense protein foods as part of a healthy dietary pattern.
  • Consume a variety of protein foods from animal sources, including eggs, poultry, seafood, and red meat, as well as plant-sourced protein foods.
  • Swap deep-fried cooking methods with baked, broiled, roasted, stir-fried, or grilled cooking methods.
  • Consume meat with no or limited added sugars, refined carbohydrates, or chemical additives.
  • Protein serving goals: 1.2â€“1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight per day.

Consume Dairy

  • Include full-fat dairy with no added sugars. Dairy is an excellent source of protein, healthy fats, vitamins, and minerals.
  • Dairy serving goals: 3 servings per day as part of a 2,000-calorie dietary pattern.

Gut Health

Your gut contains trillions of bacteria and other microorganisms called the microbiome. A healthy diet supports a well-balanced microbiome and healthy digestion. Highly processed foods can disrupt this balance, while vegetables, fruits, fermented foods, and high-fiber foods support a diverse microbiome.

Eat Vegetables & Fruits Throughout the Day

  • Eat a variety of colorful, nutrient-dense vegetables and fruits.
  • Eat whole vegetables and fruits in their original form. Wash thoroughly prior to eating raw or cooking.
  • Frozen, dried, or canned options with no or very limited added sugars can also be good choices.
  • 100% fruit or vegetable juice should be consumed in limited portions or diluted with water.
  • Serving goals: Vegetables 3 servings per day; Fruits 2 servings per day.

Incorporate Healthy Fats

  • Healthy fats are plentiful in many whole foods: meats, poultry, eggs, omega-3 seafood, nuts, seeds, full-fat dairy, olives, and avocados.
  • When cooking, prioritize oils with essential fatty acids, such as olive oil. Other options include butter or beef tallow.
  • Saturated fat consumption should not exceed 10% of total daily calories. Limiting processed foods helps meet this goal.

Focus on Whole Grains

  • Prioritize fiber-rich whole grains.
  • Significantly reduce highly processed, refined carbohydrates like white bread, ready-to-eat breakfast options, flour tortillas, and crackers.
  • Whole grains serving goals: 2â€“4 servings per day.

Limit Highly Processed Foods, Added Sugars, & Refined Carbohydrates

  • Avoid highly processed packaged foods that are salty or sweet. Prioritize nutrient-dense foods and home-prepared meals.
  • Limit foods with artificial flavors, petroleum-based dyes, artificial preservatives, and non-nutritive sweeteners.
  • Avoid sugar-sweetened beverages like sodas, fruit drinks, and energy drinks.
  • One meal should contain no more than 10 grams of added sugars.

Added Sugars

  • Look for ingredients that include "sugar" or "syrup" or end in "-ose."
  • Added sugars appear under many names: high-fructose corn syrup, agave syrup, corn syrup, fructose, glucose, dextrose, sucrose, honey, molasses, etc.
  • Naturally occurring sugars in fruits and plain milk are not considered added sugars.

Limit Alcoholic Beverages

  • Consume less alcohol for better overall health.
  • People who should completely avoid alcohol include pregnant women, those recovering from alcohol use disorder, and people on medications that interact with alcohol.

Sodium

  • Sodium and electrolytes are essential for hydration. Ages 14+: less than 2,300 mg per day.
  • Children: Ages 1â€“3: less than 1,200 mg/day; Ages 4â€“8: less than 1,500 mg/day; Ages 9â€“13: less than 1,800 mg/day.
  • Avoid highly processed foods that are high in sodium.

Infancy & Early Childhood (Birthâ€“4 Years)

  • For the first 6 months, feed your baby only breast milk. When unavailable, use iron-fortified infant formula.

  • Avoid added sugars during infancy and early childhood.

Adolescence (11â€“18 Years)

  • Rapid growth period with increased needs for energy, protein, calcium, and iron.
  • Eat nutrient-dense foods; limit sugary drinks and avoid highly processed foods.
  • Encourage participation in food shopping and cooking.

Pregnant Women

  • Pregnancy increases nutrient needs. Iron, folate, and iodine are top priorities.
  • Consume diverse nutrient-dense foods: iron-rich meats, folate-rich greens and legumes, choline-rich eggs, calcium-rich dairy, low-mercury omega-3 seafood.
  • Talk to your healthcare professional about prenatal vitamins.

Lactating Women

  • Lactation increases energy and nutrient needs.
  • Consume vitamin B12-rich proteins, omega-3 seafood, folate-rich legumes, and vitamin A-rich vegetables.

Older Adults

Some older adults need fewer calories but equal or greater amounts of protein, vitamin B12, vitamin D, and calcium. Prioritize nutrient-dense foods. When intake is insufficient, fortified foods or supplements may be needed under medical supervision.

Individuals with Chronic Disease

  • Following the Dietary Guidelines can help prevent onset or slow progression of chronic disease.
  • Individuals with certain chronic diseases may benefit from a lower carbohydrate diet. Consult your healthcare professional.

Vegetarians & Vegans

  • Consume a variety of whole, protein-rich foods: dairy, eggs, beans, peas, lentils, legumes, nuts, seeds, tofu, or tempeh.
  • Limit highly processed vegan/vegetarian foods with added fats, sugars, and salt.
  • Pay attention to nutrient gaps. Vegetarian diets often lack vitamins D and E, choline, and iron. Vegan diets show broader shortfalls.
  • Monitor nutrient status periodically, especially iron, vitamin B12, vitamin D, calcium, and iodine.
  • Prioritize targeted supplementation, diversify plant protein sources, and enhance mineral bioavailability.
